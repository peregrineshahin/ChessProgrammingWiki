 [](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sargon_%28chess%29) Sargon I for the [Apple II](Apple_II "Apple II") <a id="cite-note-1" href="#cite-ref-1">[1]</a> 
**Sargon**,  

a family of chess programs by [Dan](Dan_Spracklen "Dan Spracklen") and [Kathe Spracklen](Kathe_Spracklen "Kathe Spracklen"). They started chess programming in 1977 on a [Z-80](Z80 "Z80") based *Wavemate Jupiter III* <a id="cite-note-2" href="#cite-ref-2">[2]</a> in [assembly language](Assembly "Assembly"). The **first** Sargon version had a [10x12 board](10x12_Board#Sargon "10x12 Board") board representation and a two [ply](Ply "Ply") [alpha-beta search](Alpha-Beta "Alpha-Beta") without [quiescence](Quiescence_Search "Quiescence Search") but a [SOMA](SOMA#SOMAALGO "SOMA") like [exchange evaluation](Static_Exchange_Evaluation "Static Exchange Evaluation") <a id="cite-note-3" href="#cite-ref-3">[3]</a> <a id="cite-note-4" href="#cite-ref-4">[4]</a>. 


After first tournament successes in 1978, at [The Second West Coast Computer Faire](MCCT_1978 "MCCT 1978"), winning with a 100% score <a id="cite-note-5" href="#cite-ref-5">[5]</a>, and the shared third place at [ACM 1978](ACM_1978 "ACM 1978") already with **Sargon II**, the Spracklens became professional computer chess programmers. They first started selling their well commented Sargon I Z-80 assembly listing as paper copy for $15 by their own <a id="cite-note-6" href="#cite-ref-6">[6]</a>, and then made a contract with *Hayden Books* to publish a book about Sargon with explanations and listing <a id="cite-note-7" href="#cite-ref-7">[7]</a>. *Hayden Software* <a id="cite-note-8" href="#cite-ref-8">[8]</a> remained primary distributor of all further Sargon software versions, even when the Spracklens worked full time for [Fidelity Electronics](Fidelity_Electronics "Fidelity Electronics") during the 80s. 



## Sargon II


**Sargon II** was written for various [Z-80](Z80 "Z80") and [6502](6502 "6502") based home computers, and was base for their first [dedicated unit](Dedicated_Chess_Computers "Dedicated Chess Computers"), the [Chafitz ARB Sargon 2.5](Chafitz_ARB_Sargon_2.5 "Chafitz ARB Sargon 2.5") <a id="cite-note-10" href="#cite-ref-10">[10]</a>. After civil proceedings between manufacturer [Applied Concepts](Applied_Concepts "Applied Concepts") <a id="cite-note-11" href="#cite-ref-11">[11]</a> and their sales company [Chafitz](Chafitz "Chafitz"), the Spracklens began their long term collaboration with [Sidney Samole](Sidney_Samole "Sidney Samole") and [Fidelity Electronics](Fidelity_Electronics "Fidelity Electronics") in the 80s. Sargon II already played the [ACM 1978](ACM_1978 "ACM 1978") still with the *Jupiter III*, and the [WMCCC 1980](WMCCC_1980 "WMCCC 1980"), where it had to compete with its dedicated relatives, winner [Chess Challenger](Chess_Challenger "Chess Challenger"), runner up [Boris Experimental](Boris "Boris"), [Sargon 2.5 Modular GS](Chafitz_Modular_Game_System "Chafitz Modular Game System") and [Sargon 2.5 Auto RB](Chafitz_ARB_Sargon_2.5 "Chafitz ARB Sargon 2.5"), and became tied third with 3/5, last game losing from Challenger.




## Sargon III


**Sargon III** was a complete rewrite, using [quiescence search](Quiescence_Search "Quiescence Search"), and a [transposition table](Transposition_Table "Transposition Table") with [BCH hashing](BCH_Hashing "BCH Hashing"). Sargon III was ported to the [68000](68000 "68000"), to be the first third-party executable software for the [Macintosh](Macintosh "Macintosh") <a id="cite-note-12" href="#cite-ref-12">[12]</a>. The [8086](8086 "8086") Sargon III port for [PC](IBM_PC "IBM PC") was done by [Kevin Leavelle](index.php?title=Kevin_Leavelle&action=edit&redlink=1 "Kevin Leavelle (page does not exist)") in 1984 <a id="cite-note-13" href="#cite-ref-13">[13]</a>, so that three Sargon 3 software versions played the [WMCCC 1984](WMCCC_1984 "WMCCC 1984"), where the [Fidelity Electronics](Fidelity_Electronics "Fidelity Electronics") [Elite](Elite "Elite") by the same authors tied for first place, the [Apple II](Apple_II "Apple II") became 10th with 4/7, the Macintosh 12th with 3½/7 and the [8088](8086 "8086") [Compaq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compaq) PC 17th with 2½/7. In cooperation agreement between [Fidelity Electronics](Fidelity_Electronics "Fidelity Electronics") and [Software Toolworks](index.php?title=Software_Toolworks&action=edit&redlink=1 "Software Toolworks (page does not exist)") in 1988, an enhanced and improved Sargon III was market as [Fidelity Chessmaster 2100](Chessmaster#2100 "Chessmaster") <a id="cite-note-14" href="#cite-ref-14">[14]</a>.

### Awit


[Dan](Dan_Spracklen "Dan Spracklen") and [Kathe Spracklen](Kathe_Spracklen "Kathe Spracklen") from their oral history on the Sargon 2 vs [Awit](Awit "Awit") game, round 4, [ACM 1978](ACM_1978 "ACM 1978") <a id="cite-note-17" href="#cite-ref-17">[17]</a> <a id="cite-note-18" href="#cite-ref-18">[18]</a>:

There was like a hundred people sitting out there, watching it, and they just all started cheering and clapping. And then we woke up the following morning to a big article in the Washington Post that says, Microcomputer Beats 6 Million Dollar Machine, or something like that. 

```
