**Timothy P. (Tim) Lillicrap**,  

a Canadian [neuroscientist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuroscientist) an [AI](Artificial_Intelligence "Artificial Intelligence") researcher, adjunct professor at [University College London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_College_London), and staff research scientist at [Google](index.php?title=Google&action=edit&redlink=1 "Google (page does not exist)"), [DeepMind](index.php?title=DeepMind&action=edit&redlink=1 "DeepMind (page does not exist)"), where he is involved in the [AlphaGo](index.php?title=AlphaGo&action=edit&redlink=1 "AlphaGo (page does not exist)") and [AlphaZero](AlphaZero "AlphaZero") projects mastering the games of [Go](Go "Go"), [chess](Chess "Chess") and [Shogi](Shogi "Shogi"). He holds a B.Sc. in [cognitive science](Cognition "Cognition") and [artificial intelligence](Artificial_Intelligence "Artificial Intelligence") from [University of Toronto](University_of_Toronto "University of Toronto") in 2005, and a Ph.D. in [systems neuroscience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systems_neuroscience) from [Queen's University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Queen%27s_University) in 2014 under [Stephen H. Scott](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_H._Scott) <a id="cite-note-2" href="#cite-ref-2">[2]</a> <a id="cite-note-3" href="#cite-ref-3">[3]</a>. His research focuses on [machine learning](Learning "Learning") and [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics) for [optimal control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optimal_control) and [decision making](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decision-making), as well as using these mathematical frameworks to understand how the [brain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain) learns. He has developed algorithms and approaches for exploiting [deep neural networks](Neural_Networks#Deep "Neural Networks") in the context of [reinforcement learning](Reinforcement_Learning "Reinforcement Learning"), and new [recurrent memory architectures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recurrent_neural_network) for [one-shot learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-shot_learning) <a id="cite-note-4" href="#cite-ref-4">[4]</a>.
