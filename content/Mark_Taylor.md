**Mark Taylor**,  

a British computer chess programmer, in the 80s working for [David Levy's](David_Levy "David Levy") and [Kevin O’Connell's](Kevin_O%E2%80%99Connell "Kevin O’Connell") companies [Philidor Software](Philidor_Software "Philidor Software") and [Intelligent Software](Intelligent_Software "Intelligent Software"). Mark Taylor, a recruit from outside the computer chess tournament circuit, was a [6502](6502 "6502") expert who developed his programs on a [Commodore PET](Commodore_PET "Commodore PET"). The [SciSys](Saitek "Saitek") [Intelligent Chess](Intelligent_Chess "Intelligent Chess") <a id="cite-note-2" href="#cite-ref-2">[2]</a> computer and [PetChess](PetChess "PetChess") were mainly programmed by Mark Taylor. In an interview David Levy mentioned Mark Taylor as the most talented and genius programmer of [Intelligent Software](Intelligent_Software "Intelligent Software") <a id="cite-note-3" href="#cite-ref-3">[3]</a>.

Mark Taylor worked with Levy to produce a program for a [Hitachi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hitachi_Ltd.) 4-bit [CMOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CMOS) [microcontroller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microcontroller). The [HD44801](HMCS4xC "HMCS4xC") had 2K of 10-bit word [ROM](Memory#ROM "Memory"), **160** [nibbles](Nibble "Nibble") (80 bytes) of [RAM](Memory#RAM "Memory"), running at 400 KHz. Levy developed a chess playing algorithm for this chip which played fully legal chess including [pawn promotion](Promotions "Promotions"), [en passant capturing](En_passant "En passant"), [castling](Castling "Castling"), and even managed mate with KR v K in some versions all in 160 nibbles of RAM. A piece of work that Taylor is still rightly proud of today <a id="cite-note-4" href="#cite-ref-4">[4]</a>. The chip went into a series of small portable [SciSys](Saitek "Saitek") machines - [Mini Chess](Mini_Chess "Mini Chess"), [Junior Chess](Mini_Chess#Junior "Mini Chess") and [Graduate Chess](Mini_Chess#Graduate "Mini Chess"), all released in early 1981. The 4-bit program also initiated [Eric White's](Eric_White "Eric White") involvement in computer chess business and long time collaboration with Levy, when [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong) based manufacturer *White and Allcock*, forerunner of [Newcrest Technology](Newcrest_Technology "Newcrest Technology") introduced the CXG brand with [CXG Sensor Computachess](CXG_Sensor_Computachess "CXG Sensor Computachess") in 1981 <a id="cite-note-5" href="#cite-ref-5">[5]</a> <a id="cite-note-6" href="#cite-ref-6">[6]</a>.



## Philidor


Taylor was also involved in the [Philidor](Philidor "Philidor") project, and was responsible to translate the [Z80](Z80 "Z80") program for the SciSys [Chess Champion Mark V](Chess_Champion_Mark_V "Chess Champion Mark V") 6502 branch, also deployed for the [Milton Bradley Phantom](Milton_Bradley_Phantom "Milton Bradley Phantom"). 



## Cyrus 68K


In 1985, Taylor started to develop [Cyrus 68K](Cyrus_68K "Cyrus 68K") <a id="cite-note-7" href="#cite-ref-7">[7]</a>, which was commercially available as the [Sphinx](CXG_Sphinx "CXG Sphinx"), a [dedicated chess computer](Dedicated_Chess_Computers "Dedicated Chess Computers") under the brand name [CXG](index.php?title=Newcrest_Technolog&action=edit&redlink=1 "Newcrest Technolog (page does not exist)"), manufactured by [Newcrest Technology Ltd.](Newcrest_Technology "Newcrest Technology") <a id="cite-note-8" href="#cite-ref-8">[8]</a> <a id="cite-note-9" href="#cite-ref-9">[9]</a> <a id="cite-note-10" href="#cite-ref-10">[10]</a>.
