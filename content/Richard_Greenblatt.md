**Richard D. Greenblatt**,  

an American computer programmer, along with [Bill Gosper](Bill_Gosper "Bill Gosper") considered the co-founder of the [hacker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hacker_culture) community <a id="cite-note-2" href="#cite-ref-2">[2]</a> . At [MIT](Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology "Massachusetts Institute of Technology"), working for the [Project MAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_MAC%7CProject) (Machine-Aided Cognition), he was the main implementor of [Maclisp](index.php?title=Lisp&action=edit&redlink=1 "Lisp (page does not exist)") on the [PDP-6](PDP-6 "PDP-6") and co-developer of the [ITS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incompatible_Timesharing_System) (Incompatible Timesharing System), the operating system on which MacLisp was developed.


Richard Greenblatt was primary author of [Mac Hack](Mac_Hack "Mac Hack") (The Greenblatt Chess Program) in 1966 and [1967](Timeline#1967 "Timeline"), and along with [Tom Knight](Mathematician#TKnight "Mathematician") main designer of the [MIT Lisp machine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisp_machine). In 1978 Mac Hack got adapted to the Chess-orientated Processing System [CHEOPS](CHEOPS "CHEOPS"), one of the first dedicated hardware approaches in computer chess.


In 1979, Greenblatt founded [Lisp Machines, Inc.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisp_Machines) to build and sell [Lisp machines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisp_Machines), competing with [Symbolics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbolics), a company founded by his former [MIT AI Lab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AI_Lab) fellows around [Russell Noftsker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russell_Noftsker), [Tom Knight](Mathematician#TKnight "Mathematician") and [Jack Holloway](Jack_Holloway "Jack Holloway"). Lisp Machines, Inc. went bankrupt in 1987.
