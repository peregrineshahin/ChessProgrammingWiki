**Machiavelli**,  

a chess program developed in [1947-1948](Timeline#1948 "Timeline") by [Donald Michie](Donald_Michie "Donald Michie") and [Shaun Wylie](Shaun_Wylie "Shaun Wylie") which was a rival "paper machine" of [Alan Turing's](Alan_Turing "Alan Turing") and [David Champernowne's](David_Champernowne "David Champernowne") [Turochamp](Turochamp "Turochamp"). Turing began programming both at [University of Manchester](University_of_Manchester "University of Manchester") but never completed them and they never played each other <a id="cite-note-2" href="#cite-ref-2">[2]</a>. 


In their 1961 article *Machines that play games* <a id="cite-note-3" href="#cite-ref-3">[3]</a>, [John Maynard Smith](John_Maynard_Smith "John Maynard Smith") and [Donald Michie](Donald_Michie "Donald Michie") elaborate the [evaluation](Evaluation "Evaluation") features of their one-ply analyzers [SOMA](SOMA "SOMA") and Machiavelli, to consider [material](Material "Material"), [center](Center "Center") and [neighboring king square control](King_Safety#SquareControl "King Safety"), pieces [en-prise](En_prise "En prise"), [swap-off values](Static_Exchange_Evaluation "Static Exchange Evaluation") and other [tactical](Tactics "Tactics") and [strategical](Strategy "Strategy") considerations. Later, John Maynard Smith built a SOMA-Machiavelli hybrid named SOMAC (SOMA with features taken from the Machiavelli) <a id="cite-note-4" href="#cite-ref-4">[4]</a>. This machine, when allowed a lookahead of two, has a standard of play equal to that of a mediocre human player ... <a id="cite-note-5" href="#cite-ref-5">[5]</a> <a id="cite-note-6" href="#cite-ref-6">[6]</a> .
