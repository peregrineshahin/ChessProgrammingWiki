**Yakov Konoval**,  

a Russian chess player and programmer from [Orenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orenburg), born 1955. From 1968 until 1974, Yakov Konova studied at [Botvinnik's](Mikhail_Botvinnik "Mikhail Botvinnik") chess school <a id="cite-note-1" href="#cite-ref-1">[1]</a>. In 1982 Yakov became a programmer and soon started to combine chess and programming. In 1986-1987 he wrote a program for solving chess problems - one of the fastest for that period, and later worked on [Retrograde Analysis](Retrograde_Analysis "Retrograde Analysis") for [Endgame Tablebases](Endgame_Tablebases "Endgame Tablebases"). 



## EGTB records


In October 2005, Yakov Konoval and [Marc Bourzutschky](Marc_Bourzutschky "Marc Bourzutschky") announced that a position in the ending of a KRRNkrr requires 290 moves to convert to a simpler winning endgame <a id="cite-note-3" href="#cite-ref-3">[3]</a>. The old record was 243 moves from a position in a rook and knight versus two knights endgame, discovered by [Lewis Stiller](Lewis_Stiller "Lewis Stiller") in 1991 <a id="cite-note-4" href="#cite-ref-4">[4]</a>.


In March 2006 the wizards of 7-men endgames, [Marc Bourzutschky](Marc_Bourzutschky "Marc Bourzutschky") and Yakov Konoval found a 330 moves win in KQBNkqb <a id="cite-note-5" href="#cite-ref-5">[5]</a> and in May 2006 a 517 moves win in KQNkrbn <a id="cite-note-6" href="#cite-ref-6">[6]</a> <a id="cite-note-7" href="#cite-ref-7">[7]</a>.
